Test 2 – Signals, Digital Data

Introduction

You will learn to describe signals/digital data in naval communications.

Objectives

  • Describe the types of signals in RF communications
  • Identify the types of digital data
  • Describe importance of timing of digital data
  • Describe importance of handshaking signals for digital data processing
  • Describe difference between AF, IF, and RF signal types
  • Identify types of equipment that convert digital data to RF signals

Analog vs Digital

Analog is curved/continuous. Digital is 1s and 0s.

Signals Transmit Data

Signals are the basis of all RF comms. Equipment used in RF (satellites, antennas, modems, transceivers, computers) accepts a signal and transforms it into another format so it can be read by another piece of equipment. Both analog and digital signals are used, but most devices use digital because it is easier to transmit and is more reliable.

Analog Signals are Continuous and Constantly Varying

Analog uses electricity to transmit data by creating a continuous/varying wave. Think of a dimmer switch: many variations of brightness. Analog signals are subject to noise (unwanted electrical/electromagnetic energy) that degrades signal quality. Noise can come from A/C units, fluorescent lights, and magnetic fields.

Equipment – Analog

  • High Frequency Radio Group voice circuit
  • Digital to analog converters (DAC)
  • Analog switchboards
  • Ship’s phone lines

Digital Signals are Discrete and Discontinuous

Digital signals are electrical impulses in a discontinuous stream of on/off pulses (1s and 0s). They are generally more accurate/quick than analog. Digital encoding increases the capacity of a given radio frequency.

Equipment – Digital

  • Multiplexers
  • Data Terminal Equipment (DTE): computer terminal / PC
  • Data Communications Equipment (DCE): modem

Key Differences (Analog vs Digital)

  • Signal quality: analog degrades and picks up static/noise over distance; digital tends to remain “perfect” until drop-out.
  • Infrastructure: analog often needs complex, high-power amplifiers (can distort); digital can use regenerators to rebuild the signal.
  • Capacity: digital supports compression and encryption, increasing throughput and security.

Advantages of Digital

  • Recordings do not degrade over time; reading the same numbers reproduces the same waveform.
  • Data can often be compressed by finding patterns.
  • Digital data is easily encrypted.

Digital Data is Binary

Digital data is a combination of 1s and 0s.

Synchronization of Digital Communications

Computers and comm equipment (modems, multiplexers, etc.) can send/receive digital data in coordinated or uncoordinated methods.

Synchronous (SYNC) Data

SYNC data is transmitted at a rate determined by an associated clock. Synchronous transmission is fast and reliable when a sender must have a response before continuing, but “blocking” behavior can be unacceptable in some critical applications.

Asynchronous (ASYNC) Data

ASYNC data is transmitted without a clock, but uses start/stop bits (usually low-speed). Receiving equipment detects data using start/stop bits at the beginning and ending of five to eight data bits.

Isochronous (ISOC) Data

ISOC data is transmitted without a clock and without start/stop bits. It uses oversampling to ensure proper data bits are received. Multimedia streams often require isochronous transport so audio stays synchronized with video.

Digital Transmission Mode – Helper (ISOC, ASYNC, SYNC)

  • SYNC: like a marching band (lockstep, constant external clock).
  • ASYNC: like people texting (start/stop bits per character; simple but slower due to overhead).
  • ISOC: like a flowing water pipe (steady stream; essential for real-time voice/video).

Importance of Digital Timing

Data transmitted through SATCOM systems is digital. Before transmission, clock/timing info is associated with data during modulation. Timing info allows transmitting equipment (e.g., modem) to synchronize with receiving equipment (receiving modem).

Example: If the record (data) spins at a different speed than the needle (receiver clock), the output is gibberish. Sync ensures both run at the same speed so the message is clear.

Clocks in Digital Transmission

Clock sources are typically atomic clocks (e.g., Cesium). The steady decay rate provides a constant reference for accurate timing.

Accuracy of Clock Sources (Strata)

To minimize bit errors, all digital devices in a comm network must sync to a master timing source. Accuracy levels are called strata.

  • Stratum 1: SHF SATCOM systems
  • Stratum 2: EHF SATCOM systems
  • Stratum 3: HF and UHF SATCOM systems

Clocks and Data Relationships

Clock pulses are generally half the width of data bits. Clock transitions trigger the digital system to sample the data line and interpret binary 1 or 0.

  • Co-directional: clocks and data signals come from the same source.
  • Counter-directional: clocks and data signals come from opposite sources.

Handshaking Signals Coordinate Data Transfer

Handshaking signals are dedicated lines used to coordinate data transfer (ready/not ready, acknowledgements). In ASYNC, handshaking avoids sending data before the receiver is ready. Handshaking is mandatory.

Audio, Intermediate, and Radio Frequencies in SATCOM

Navy SATCOM uses RF, IF, and AF in many circuits. When receiving, the chain typically converts from RF → IF → DC (digital current/data) → AF. When transmitting, the chain is reversed. Data circuits do not contain AF.

DirectionPathProcess
Receive (RX) RF → IF → DC → AF High-speed space waves are stepped down to baseband data or audio.
Transmit (TX) AF → DC → IF → RF Audio or data is stepped up to high-frequency waves for space travel.
  • RF (Radio Frequency): high-energy carrier wave for atmosphere/space.
  • IF (Intermediate Frequency): “middle” frequency for easier processing and filtering.
  • DC (Digital Current/Data): raw 1s and 0s extracted from the signal.
  • AF (Audio Frequency): final stage—sound for voice circuits (omitted for pure data circuits).

No AF needed: if sending a file/message (data circuit), the chain stops at DC.

Characteristics of Audio Frequency

  • AF is mechanical (not an electromagnetic wave).
  • AF requires a medium (air, water, conductive wire); it cannot travel through space.
  • AF requires a transmitter and receiver.

Intermediate Frequency as a Processing “Mediator”

IF allows radio systems to process signals more effectively. High frequency signals are often converted to a lower IF for stable amplification/filtering. In tunable receivers, IF converts various station frequencies to a common frequency for processing.

Communication Happens Through Frequency Conversions

On ships, digital data passes through ADNS routers to baseband equipment for encryption. Then it goes to Below Deck Equipment (BDE) to be modulated into IF, and then to Above Deck Equipment (ADE) where it is converted to RF for SATCOM. Signals received from SATCOM also convert down to IF before demodulation.

Radio Waves Carry Voice or Data

RF is an electromagnetic wave and requires a transmitter (creates RF) and a receiver (receives RF). Navy comms are predominantly RF-based because RF supports long-range, battle group comms, teletype/data/voice/SATCOM, navigation, comms with subs, and mobile comms.

DTE and DCE Convert Signals

Data Terminal Equipment (DTE)

  • PC terminals (computers)
  • Multiplexer aggregates
  • Cryptographic equipment transmit interfaces

DTE is the functional unit that serves as a data source or sink and provides data comm control per link protocol.

Data Communications Equipment (DCE)

  • Modems
  • Multiplexer ports
  • Cryptographic equipment receive interfaces

DCE performs signal conversion, coding, and line clocking. A modem is the most common DCE.

Summary

  • Analog systems degrade differently than digital; digital can be rebuilt/regenerated.
  • Digital data transmission types: ASYNC, ISOC, SYNC.
  • Clock/timing information synchronizes transmit and receive equipment for accurate communications.
  • Handshaking is mandatory (especially for ASYNC) to coordinate transfer.
  • Understanding AF vs IF vs RF matters because each supports different capabilities in the comm chain.
  • DTE generates outgoing data; DCE accepts signals for transmission and performs conversion/coding/clocking.
