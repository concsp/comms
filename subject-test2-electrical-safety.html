Test 2 – Electrical Safety

What is Electrical Shock

An electrical shock occurs when an electric current passes through the body, which becomes part of a closed circuit – is the sensation and muscular spasm caused when electric current passes through the body.

Effects of Current

All living cells contain electrolytes and carry electric current. However electrical current from outside the body can cause injuries and death when it passes through the body. Harmful currents are produced by varying amounts of voltage. Amount of damage the current does to the body depends on the resistance of the circuit thru the living tissue.

Amount and Path

There are four factors that determine shock severity

  • Amount: the amount of current that flows thru body, higher current will increase:
    • Potential for large amounts of current to flow thru body
    • Severity of shock
  • Path: the path current takes thru body, two most dangerous paths are from
    • Hand to hand
    • Left hand to either foot (the most dangerous since current flows thru both heart and other vital organs)

Resistance and Time

Two other factors that determine shock severity are:

  • Resistance
    • The amount of body resistance present compared to current flow.
    • Body res varies greatly in dif parts/under dif conditions
    • Sweat is an excellent conductor, so contact with sweat as a factor offer less resistance
  • Time
    • Length of time the current flows thru body, longer you are shocked:
    • Greater chance the heart will fibrillate. Most people die from fibrillation.
    • Less current needed to cause fibrillation.

Avoiding Electrical Shock

Isolate the Source

Isolate source of electricity by separating it from conducting bodies. Best practices include:

  • Securing power before removing/maintaining elec. equipment
  • Keeping covers/doors/enclosures in place or closed when elec. Equipment not being used.

Isolate Exposed Live Circuitry

When working on live circuitry, isolate source by:

  • Getting permission to work on live equip. from CO (Energ. Equip. Chit)
  • Rope off area
  • Post signs
  • Warn workers of danger
  • Remove metal/conductive objects from person
  • Wear PPE
  • Do NOT work alone

Insulate the Source

Insulating means to cover/line/separate source with a material that prevent/reduce transfer of electricity. Best practices to insulate sources:

  • Ensure tools are properly insulated and checked for safety by Tool PO (Electricians Mate)
  • Use only insulated hand/portable electric power tools
  • Freq. check power/extension cords for deterioration, cracks or breaks.

Ground the Source

Electricity follows the path of least resistance. To prevent shock ensure equipment used is well grounded. Grounded equipment directs stray current to ground and away from the user. A good ground ALSO protects equipment from voltage spikes/lightning.

RADHAZ - Radiation Hazards

Two main sources of RADHAZ are:

  • Antennas
  • Lasers

Antennas and RF Burn

Radiation from antennas fed by high-power RF (Radio Frequency) transmitterse has the potential to burn personnel who are touching the antenna. An RF burn is result of current flowing thru body when parts are in contact with RF voltages. The current produces heat as it passes thru the skin, causing effects from warmth to painful burns. The eyes/other organs are extremely sensitive to RF radiation.

Effects of Lasers

The effects of a laser include damager to the:

  • Eyes, ranging from cornea inflammation to corneal burn and retinal damage.
  • Skin, ranging from accelerated aging to skin burn.

Laser stands for (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation)

HERO - Hazards of Electromagnetic Radiation

HERO are hazards caused by presence of electromagnetic radiation in excess of established safety levels NEAR ordnance.

Example: a transmitter antenna can cause an electromagnetic field strong enough to:

  • Generate currents and/or voltages large enough to:
    • Prematurely fire the ordnance
    • Cause the ordnance to be rendered ineffective (dudding) by damaging its electro-explosive devices or weapons’ components
  • Harm or injure humans and wildlife

Implementing HERO Requirements

The CO of each ship is responsible for implementing HERO requirements. The CO must establish a procedure to control radiation from radio antennas by issuing a command instruction called the HERO Bill or the HERO/EMCON Bill.

HERO Precaution

Following are required under HERO conditions:

  • Follow set procedure to prevent transmitters from sending out RF signals.
  • Secure radio/radar transmitters, place into a dummy load, place in standby or turn off.
  • Place non-essential communication systems in standby/off.
  • Make announcements over the ship’s intercom (1MC) to set or cancel HERO/EMCON condition.
  • Specific guidance may differ per ship based on that ship’s HERO survey

Tag-out System Overview

The tag-out system uses tags and labels to indicate the safety status of potentially dangerous equipment. Purpose of the system is to provide procedures for personnel to use:

  • For preventing improper operation of a component, piece of equipment, system or portion of a system that is isolated in an abnormal condition.
  • In operating an instrument th at is unreliable or not in normal operating condition.
  • When accomplishing PMS (Planned Maintenance System) actions.

Tags and Labels

The following tags/labels are attached to switches, circuit breakers, equipment, systems, or plumbing valves to indicate they should be operating with care/left alone.

  • Yellow CAUTION tags
  • Red DANGER tags
    • Jeopardize safety of personnel.
  • Orange OUT-OF-CALIBRATION tags
  • Purple OUT-OF-COMMISSION tags

Adding a tag to equipment is called “tagging out” the item.

Tag-out Log

A permanent log of authorizations given for all tag-out actions.

Types of Hazards (Working Aloft)

Aloft = over the side of the ship. Greatest danger is falling, others include:

  • Falling objects or tools
  • Radiation burns
  • Suffocation

Permission for Going Aloft

Never go aloft on masts, stacks, or be suspended over the side of a ship by a crane without first getting permission. Even though permission is a long process it is essential. Man Aloft Chit.

Safety Equipment for Aloft

Always use PPE:

  • Wear air respirators near stacks/exhausts
  • Safety harness
  • Ensure all equipment is secured to prevent injury to personnel that may occur (falling tools/equipment)

Verbal Aloft Warnings

1MC messages for members working aloft every 15 minutes.

Safety Observer for Working Aloft

Position a safety observer on deck near the work, their job is to:

  • Make sure personnel not involved in the work are not standing in the deck area beneath the work.
  • Call for help and be able to properly/safely go aloft to assist Sailor in distress
  • Be fully prepared and outfitted with a safety harness, lanyards and climber safety sleeve to permit rapid emergency assistance aloft if required.
  • NEVER work aloft without an observer.

RADHAZ while Aloft

To avoid RADHAZ when working aloft:

  • Make sure all transmitters and radar that pose RADHAZ are in STANDBY position, or tag the item or have someone in POSCON (Positive Control standing near controls of radar).
  • Place a sign on them that reads “SECURED PERSONNEL ALOFT” and includes date, time, and initials of the person working aloft.

Electrical Safety Shoes/Rubber Insulating Gloves

Wearing proper PPE is essential in the Navy. You may be issued electrical safety shoes, they have special non-conducting soles designed to protect you from shock, rated to insulate up to a maximum of 600 volts. Rubber insulating gloves are divided into four classes, (0, 1, 2, and 3) based on wall thickness of gloves and their maximum safe voltage rating.

Required Eye/Hearing Protection

NAVOSH (Naval Occupational Safety and Health) Program states that you are required to wear appropriate protecting when performing hazardous operation (eye PRO for eye hazards, ear PRO for hearing hazards)

84 Decibels or higher is enough to damage hearing.

Proper Respiratory Protection

Use respiratory protection when working with HAZMAT (hazardous materials) such as paints, solvents, materials for cleaning/maintenance, and also:

  • Chip lead or chromate-based paints while removing corrosion
  • Prime and paint the bases of an antenna
  • Clean circuits with solvents/alcohol

Safety Precautions

  • Never work alone
  • Never shock yourself on purpose
  • Work on, operate, or adjust equipment only if authorized
  • Avoid working on energized equipment unless absolutely necessary
  • Keep loose tools, metal parts, and liquids away from electrical equipment
  • Never use steel wool or emery cloth on electric/electronic circuits
  • Never attempt to repair energized circuits unless in an emergency.
  • Use proper tag-out procedures for regular/preventative maintenance
  • Be cautious when working in unventilated spaces
  • Never attempt to stop a rotating antenna manually
  • Keep protective closures, fuse panels, and circuit breaker boxes closed unless you are working on them.
  • Safety is always paramount
