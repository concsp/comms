Test 2 – Radio Frequency Theory

Objectives

  • Identify factors that impact radio wave propagation
  • Identify the effects of atmospheric conditions on radio waves
  • Describe three propagation behaviors of radio waves: reflection, refraction, diffraction
  • Define the three types of critical radio wave frequencies: LUF, FOT, MUF
  • Describe the cause of attenuation in radio waves
  • Describe three types of radio waves as defined by propagation paths: surface, space, sky waves

Effects on Radio Wave Propagation

Above the atmosphere very little affects propagation. Within atmospheric layers, common influences include:

  • Weather conditions
  • Time of day
  • Geographic location

Weather

  • Inclement weather (rain/thunderstorms) can increase interference.
  • Clear weather stabilizes conditions and can reduce interference.

Day and Night

Time of day affects waves because the sun’s ultraviolet radiation impacts ionization levels (charged particles) in the atmosphere.

  • Daytime: more ionization from UV → more interference.
  • Nighttime: decreased ionization → less interference.

Location

Geographic location can affect propagation. Near the North/South poles, increased magnetic interference can create comms problems.

Three Propagation Behaviours

As waves move through layers of the atmosphere, behavior commonly falls into three categories:

  • Reflection
  • Refraction
  • Diffraction

Reflection

Reflection occurs when radio waves bounce from a flat surface such as:

  • The Earth
  • Metallic surfaces
  • The ionosphere (uppermost layer)

Waves transmitted at an angle can reflect off the Earth’s surface and travel toward a receiving antenna.

Refraction

Refraction changes the direction radio waves travel when waves bend as they pass from one medium to another with different characteristics (e.g., temperature, pressure, humidity, density).

Memory: Like a straw looking bent in a glass of water.

Diffraction

Diffraction occurs when radio waves hit a solid/dense object and bend around it, changing direction. Diffraction enables comms between two points that are not in a direct path (an obstacle blocks line of sight).

Memory: Like water flowing around a rock in a stream.

Frequency Effects

Three important/critical frequencies:

  • LUF: Lowest Usable Frequency
  • FOT: Frequency of Optimum Transmission
  • MUF: Maximum Usable Frequency (also known as critical frequency)

Propagation Paths

Two broad path directions:

  • Direct: follows the Earth’s surface
  • Indirect: travels through the atmosphere

Absorption of Energy (Attenuation)

Radio waves traveling over a surface lose some energy as the surface absorbs it. The decrease in signal strength is called attenuation (absorption).

Conductive Surfaces Decrease Attenuation

  • Less conductive terrain (rocky/jungle/ground) absorbs more energy → more attenuation.
  • Water is more conductive → less energy loss during propagation.

Surface, Space, and Sky Waves

  • Surface waves follow the curvature of the Earth.
  • Space waves travel directly from antenna to antenna (line of sight).
  • Sky waves travel into the atmosphere and are refracted in the ionosphere, returning to Earth at longer distances and being less affected by attenuation.

RF vs AF (Audio Frequency)

RF is electromagnetic and AF is mechanical. Both can share the same numerical frequency, but:

  • RF: electromagnetic waves that travel through a vacuum.
  • AF: mechanical sound waves that require a medium (air/water) to vibrate through.

Memory Triggers

  • USE: We USE satellites for high-speed data — UHF, SHF, EHF.
  • As you move from ELF → EHF: wavelength decreases, energy increases, antennas get smaller, LOS becomes more critical.
  • Mnemonic: “Even Very Loud Music Has Very Unusual Side Effects.”

ELF to EHF

  • Wavelength decreases
  • Energy increases

Long-Range Communications

Lower-end frequencies (ELF, VLF, LF) are used for:

  • Long range comms
  • Communications with submarines below the surface
  • Navigation systems

Numerous Applications and Mobile

Higher-end frequencies (HF, VHF, UHF, SHF, EHF) are used for:

  • Teletype, data, voice, and satellite comms
  • Mobile communications

Reminder: Think USE — we use UHF/SHF/EHF for high-speed data.

Distance Ranges (HF, VHF, UHF)

  • HF: ~2000 miles (depends on atmospheric conditions); surface comms; susceptible to jamming.
  • VHF: 25–35 nm surface; 150–300 nm airborne; portable; supports amphibious ops.
  • UHF: 25–35 nm surface; 150–300 nm airborne; beyond atmosphere for SATCOM; supports commercial TV broadcasts.

Characteristics of SHF / EHF

  • SHF: beyond-atmosphere SATCOM; reliable, high-capacity, large bandwidth comms.
  • EHF: worldwide SATCOM coverage; increased capacity and jam resistance; nuclear survivable.
